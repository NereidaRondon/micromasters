Androgogy

Andragogy is a learning theory that focuses on the unique needs and characteristics of adult learners. It was first proposed by Malcolm Knowles in the late 1960s and early 1970s, based on his work with adult learners in a variety of settings (Knowles, 1970; Merriam & Bierema, 2014).

The assumptions of Androgogy are:
  • Adults need to be involved in the planning and evaluation of their instruction. (Knowles, 1984, p. 12)
  • Experience (including mistakes) provides the basis for learning activities. (Knowles, 1984, p. 18)
  • Adults are more self-directed and assume greater responsibility for their learning compared to children. (Knowles, 1984, p. 18)
  • Adults are most interested in learning subjects that have immediate relevance and impact to their job or personal life. (Knowles, 1984, p. 19)
  • Adult learning is problem-centered rather than content-oriented. (Knowles, 1984, p. 26)

In terms of its strengths and limitations, andragogy has been praised for its focus on the unique needs and characteristics of adult learners, which can help to create more effective and engaging learning experiences for this population (Merriam & Bierema, 2014). However, some have criticized andragogy for being too individualistic and not taking into account the social and cultural factors that can impact adult learning. For example, Fenwick (2001) argues that the theory places too much emphasis on the individual and not enough on the social context in which learning occurs. She suggests that adult learners are embedded in complex social and cultural systems that can shape their learning experiences in profound ways, and that these factors must be taken into account when designing educational programs.

Andragogy has important implications for instructional design, as it emphasizes the need to create learning experiences that are tailored to the needs and interests of adult learners. This includes understanding the characteristics and motivations of adult learners, and designing relevant and engaging learning experiences that focus on problem-solving, critical thinking, and application to real-world situations, rather than focusing solely on the content to be taught. This might include opportunities for self-directed learning, problem-based learning, and application to real-world situations (Merriam & Bierema, 2014). Additionally, andragogy highlights the importance of creating a supportive and collaborative learning environment, where adult learners can draw on their own experiences and engage in dialogue with peers (Brookfield, 1986). By incorporating these principles into instructional design, educators and trainers can create effective and meaningful learning experiences for adult learners.

In my current position as a Learning Content Developer for a Software Development Boot Camp, I see first hand the importance of Andragogy princicples and their effectiveness in producing engaging professional development programs for adult learners. For example, we incorporate opportunities for self-directed learning by implementing a flipped classroom model. A majority of the weekly projects allow students to choose what project to work on and creative freedom on the project concept and layout. As a whole, the program promotes collaboration among boot camp students, mentors, and alumni through the use of discussion forums and cohort Slack channels. Furthermore, students' previous experiences surface in their choice of projects. Adult learners pick a subject that is relevant to their current or former job positions or one that solves a problem that is personal to them in some way. Like any course, there is plenty of content but I find that the majority of the learning comes from the unavoidable struggle of coding that comes with the hands-on approach to learning software development.
