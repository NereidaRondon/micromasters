Summary A person's environment stimulus determines behavior, not what is in the person's mind. Learning is an active process of constructing knowledge through personal experiences and interactions with the environment.
  • Emphasizes the importance of prior knowledge and existing mental models
  • Teaching strategies include collaborative learning, problem-based learning, and scaffolding
  • Criticisms include lack of attention to the role of the teacher and potential for misinterpretation or misapplication of constructivist principles

 Learning occurs through the acquisition and organization of information in the mind, and that learners actively construct knowledge.
  • Emphasizes the role of mental processes such as memory, attention, and problem-solving in learning.
  • Teaching strategies include guided discovery, problem-solving, and inquiry-based learning
  • Criticisms include overemphasis on individual cognition and lack of attention to social and cultural factors
    Learning depends on the unique characteristics and needs of adult learners.
  • Emphasizes self-directed learning, relevance to personal and professional goals, and prior experience
  • Teaching strategies include problem-based learning, real-world application, and collaborative learning
  • Criticisms include potential for over-reliance on individualism and lack of attention to cultural diversity and social factors
Educator's Role The educator's role is to provide clear and consistent stimuli to elicit a desired response from the learner. The educator's role is to facilitate the learner's construction of knowledge by providing opportunities for exploration, discovery, and problem-solving. The educator's role is to facilitate the learner's cognitive processes by providing appropriate learning activities and strategies. The educator's role is to facilitate the learner's self-directed learning by providing guidance and resources as needed.
Learner's Role The learner is seen as a passive recipient of information, who responds to the stimuli presented by the educator. The learner is an active participant in the learning process, who constructs their own understanding of the world based on their experiences and interactions with their environment. The learner is an active participant in the learning process, who uses their existing knowledge and mental processes to make sense of new information. The learner is an autonomous, self-directed individual who takes responsibility for their own learning.
Similarities Shares with the other learning theories the goal of changing behavior, and the use of reinforcement to encourage or discourage certain behaviors. Shares with other learning theories the goal of changing behavior, but also emphasizes the learner's active role in constructing their own knowledge. Shares with other learning theories the goal of changing behavior, but also emphasizes the learner's mental processes and the role of prior knowledge in learning. Shares with other learning theories the goal of changing behavior, but also emphasizes the learner's active role in the learning process and the importance of relevance to the learner's personal and professional goals.
Differences Differs from other learning theories in its focus on observable behavior, and its disregard for the learner's internal mental processes. Differs from other learning theories in its rejection of the idea that knowledge can be transmitted directly from the educator to the learner, and its emphasis on the social and cultural context of learning. Differs from other learning theories in its focus on the learner's internal mental processes, and its emphasis on memory, attention, and problem-solving. Differs from other learning theories in its focus on adult learners, who are seen as more autonomous and self-directed than children and adolescents, and its emphasis on the importance of experience and prior knowledge in adult learning.
